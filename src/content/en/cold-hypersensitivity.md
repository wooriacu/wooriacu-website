Cold hypersensitivity refers to a state where an individual feels an excessive, uncomfortable chill in their hands or feet, even in environments where others feel perfectly comfortable. This sensation can persist regardless of the season. In many cases, this coldness extends to the knees, lower abdomen, and lower back, signaling a broader systemic imbalance.

## 1. Contributing Factors and Characteristics

- **Autonomic Regulation:** Cold hypersensitivity is often linked to a hyper-reactive sympathetic nervous system. When triggered by external cold or emotional stress, blood vessels constrict excessively, reducing flow to the extremities.

- **Hormonal and Structural Factors:** Women—particularly those in postpartum stages or experiencing menopausal transitions—often encounter these symptoms due to hormonal shifts and lower basal metabolic rates, which affect vascular regulation.

- **Note on Evaluation:** While often constitutional, cold extremities can be an early indicator of underlying conditions like Raynaud's phenomenon or thyroid imbalances. Rather than relying solely on self-management, we recommend a professional evaluation to understand your specific vascular and metabolic profile.

## 2. Essential Daily Management

- **Core Warmth:** Keep the entire body warm with layers rather than focusing only on the extremities.

- **Systemic Circulation:** Regular light aerobic exercise and lower-body resistance training help enhance the body's capacity to generate internal heat.

- **Stress Modulation:** Since stress triggers sympathetic nerve dominance, deep abdominal breathing or mindfulness can help relax the nervous system and mitigate vascular constriction.

- **Dietary Habits:** Prioritize warm, nourishing meals and avoid excessively cold foods or beverages to support digestive vitality and inner circulation.
